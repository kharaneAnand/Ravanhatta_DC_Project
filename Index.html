About the Ravanhatta

The Ravanhatta (or Rawanhattha) is one of the oldest string instruments of India. Its origin dates back to ancient times and is associated with King Ravana from the epic Ramayana. According to legend, Ravana created this instrument as an offering to Lord Shiva.

This bowed stringed instrument is primarily played in Rajasthan, Gujarat, and parts of Pakistan. It produces a haunting, resonant sound that is integral to the folk music traditions of these regions.

The instrument consists of a bowl-shaped resonator covered with goat skin, a wooden neck, and strings typically made from horsehair or steel. The bow (called "gaz") is usually made from bamboo or wood and horsehair.

Sound Bowl

The resonating chamber usually made from a hollowed coconut shell covered with goat skin.

Neck

A wooden rod often decorated with intricate carvings that holds the strings.

Tuning Pegs

Wooden pegs used to adjust the tension and tuning of the strings.

Bridge

Supports the strings and transfers vibrations to the sound bowl.

Strings

Traditionally made from gut or horsehair, modern versions may use metal strings. Usually has 1-2 main strings and several sympathetic strings.

Bow (Gaz)

A curved piece of wood with horsehair strung between its ends, used to play the instrument by drawing across the strings.

History & Cultural Significance

Ancient Origins

The Ravanhatta is believed to have originated over 5000 years ago. According to Hindu mythology, it was created by King Ravana as an offering to Lord Shiva.

Regional Variations

The instrument spread throughout Northwestern India and is especially popular in Rajasthan, where it became an integral part of folk music traditions.

Cultural Significance

Often used by wandering bards to accompany epic stories and folk tales. The instrument plays a crucial role in preserving oral traditions.

Modern Revival

Contemporary musicians have begun incorporating the Ravanhatta into fusion music, bringing this ancient instrument to global audiences.
